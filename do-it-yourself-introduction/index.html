Do It Yourself: Introduction

Serge, 
November 9th, 2019 · 4 min read

When looking around for available “learn to code” materials, I cannot help but remember this:

There are millions of posts and videos which explain what the linked list is and how to find loops in it with constant storage. There also seem to be enough pro-grade material (conference talks, tons of open source code of varying quality, even peers at work after you’ve got to a “hirable” level). And between these two — The Void. Apparently, I’m not the only one who noticed this. There is a great article that goes into deep details about this. Here’s an illustration from it that shows this phenomenon:

Author introduces a notion of “desert of despair” - the moment when all low hanging fruits are already picked and you are a world class linked-list reverser, but anything beyond that is cryptic, unapproachable and confusing. This is due to a fact that scope of the required knowledge increases rapidly while amount of learning material and guidance as rapidly approaches zero. What few materials that are present often lack perspective: concentrating on concrete steps in very specific scenarios instead of teaching interconnected mesh of concepts, in other words prioritizing mindless memorization over understanding.

Here’s a concrete example: Simplified breadth-first search is one of two external links on the wikipedia’s BFS page. This is what “simplified” apparently means these days:

Given a graph G = (V, E) and a distinguished source vertex s, breadth-first search systematically explores the edges of G to “discover” every vertex that is reachable from s. It computes the distance (smallest number of edges) from s to each reachable vertex. It also produces a “breadth-first tree” with root s that contains all reachable vertices. For any vertex reachable from s, the simple path in the breadth-first tree from s to corresponds to a “shortest path” from s to v in G, that is, a path containing the smallest number of edges. The algorithm works on both directed and undirected graphs.

I’m not trying to point fingers - I’m fully aware that some of my own ramblings are as bad as these. And these aren’t bad because they are factually incorrect - on the contrary, it even mentions spanning tree within a graph, that is often missed. It’s just I cannot figure out target audience for that article. Anyone who has enough fluency in the lingo to understand the “simplified” description already knows graph theory fundamentals, for the rest it has same value as if it was written in a foreign language. What does 0⟶2⟶1⟶3⟶4⟶5 have to do with “breadth” as opposed to 0⟶2⟶3⟶4⟶1⟶5 which is obviously “depth-first”? Why 2 is visited before 1? What happens in Step 1? While all these questions have reasonably logical answers, it’s not a level of clarity I’d like to see anywhere let alone in a “simplified” learning materials.

Another great example of clarity being slaughtered by ruthless engineers is CAP-consistency vs ACID-consistency. CAP stands for “consistency, availability, partition tolerance”, while ACID is “atomicity, consistency, isolation, durability”. Both abbreviations reflect really important ideas within same domain - data storage. One would expect that since “consistency” appears in both of them and is used in the same domain it would be consistent if these two actually meant the same thing. One would be wrong.

And here’s one from my personal experience. A little while back I’ve been fooling around with Apache Kafka and at one point I needed to know the answer to a simple question: does Kafka guarantee a total ordering of events inside a topic? If you think that I’m stupid to even ask such a question - obviously there’s no total ordering between shards/partitions in a distributed system, think again: Facebook’s LogDevice (in which “records” are roughly equivalent to “events” in Kafka, and “logs” are similar to Kafka’s “topics”) provides such guarantees and more per “log” while still maintaining pretty impressive scalability. Clearly, it’s a design decision and I just wanted to know what decision Kafka’s developers made. Also, if you think that I’m stupid because after 70 years of experience with production Kafka deployments the answer is intuitive to you — it’s not good either: sometimes “you have to know X in order to learn X” doesn’t cut it.

It took me several days to figure it out (on and off — I’ve done it in my spare time while also using that spare time for other things). Kafka’s design docs don’t shed any light on it, I hoped that since Kafka Streams are layered on top of actual Kafka some docs over there may give some hints — but no luck. Didn’t find anything on stackoverflow. So I had to watch over a dozen 1-2 hour presentations on youtube until one video (and I don’t even remember which one) answered my question in passing. Meanwhile I’ve been barraged with all kinds of non-interconnected information: which command line switches to use in a dozen specific situations without really explaining why, fact that you need to configure X partitions but not Y - without explaining why, the fact that number of consumer groups should not exceed number of partitions (I think this was explained couple times — but majority of talks didn’t even try). I understand the challenge of packing as much digestible information as possible into 1 hour talk and I don’t blame any of the presenters (most of them are simply awesome). But the problem remains - how do I understand something as big as Kafka (or Cassandra, or various blockchain technologies, or all the machine learning hype, or search engines, or compression technologies, or, or, or) without spending years diving deep into each one? Well, let me borrow the quote

What I cannot create, I do not understand

— Richard Feynman

…and put it upside down: in order to understand something, let’s create it. My goal here is to recreate various pieces of tech as close to the original design as possible, while focusing on a primary goal — readability. All optimizations, corner-case scenarios, compatibility and even graceful error-handling (as long as I don’t write specifically about error handling) is a “non-essential complexity” for me and I will try to remove it to the best of my ability.

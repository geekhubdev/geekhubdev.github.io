Sector f7, math and self-limiting beliefs

Serge, 
October 14th, 2019 · 4 min read

One of the lesser known real stories that seem too good to be true is a story of George Dantzig, who solved “unsolvable” mathematical problems just because he didn’t know they are “unsolvable”:

It happened because during my first year at Berkeley I arrived late one day at one of [Jerzy] Neyman’s classes. On the blackboard there were two problems that I assumed had been assigned for homework. I copied them down. A few days later I apologized to Neyman for taking so long to do the homework — the problems seemed to be a little harder than usual. I asked him if he still wanted it. He told me to throw it on his desk. I did so reluctantly because his desk was covered with such a heap of papers that I feared my homework would be lost there forever. About six weeks later, one Sunday morning about eight o’clock, [my wife] Anne and I were awakened by someone banging on our front door. It was Neyman. He rushed in with papers in hand, all excited: “I’ve just written an introduction to one of your papers. Read it so I can send it out right away for publication.” For a minute I had no idea what he was talking about. To make a long story short, the problems on the blackboard that I had solved thinking they were homework were in fact two famous unsolved problems in statistics. That was the first inkling I had that there was anything special about them.

A year later, when I began to worry about a thesis topic, Neyman just shrugged and told me to wrap the two problems in a binder and he would accept them as my thesis.

The second of the two problems, however, was not published until after World War II. It happened this way. Around 1950 I received a letter from Abraham Wald enclosing the final galley proofs of a paper of his about to go to press in the Annals of Mathematical Statistics. Someone had just pointed out to him that the main result in his paper was the same as the second “homework” problem solved in my thesis. I wrote back suggesting we publish jointly. He simply inserted my name as coauthor into the galley proof.

I remember first time, when I realized that doing “impossible” things are sometimes easier than it looks. It’s not quite as impressive as the story above, but it taught me a lesson and therefore it’s valuable.

My first computer didn’t have a disk drive at all. Second one… didn’t have it either. Third one was a soviet clone of ZX-Spectrum, that had a 5.25” floppy disk drive controlled by a soviet clone of a FD1793 disk controller. Before you can use any disk it should have been “formatted”. On a controller level it has been achieved by writing “write track” command into a command register and then writing “markup” for the entire track into a data register as described here. Below is a summary:

When controller receives values 00-f4, f8-ff it just writes them down to a disk as is, byte values f5-f7 had special meaning. It’s somewhat similar to C-style printf format string in which most symbols are just printed as is, except for \ and % which have special meaning. In this case 3 * f5 indicate beginning of either an index (or header) block which contains metadata (track/side/sector numbers) or a data block, which contains, well, user data itself. Sending f7 indicates end of header or data block and writes down a 16-bit CRC checksum to verify integrity. So, since one needed to write down a sector number while writing a “header” block and controller has a special treatment for bytes f5-f7, it’s been common knowledge that it is impossible to have sectors with those numbers. In fact, datasheet clearly states:

However, if the FD179X detects a data pattern of F5 thru FE in the data register, this is interpreted as data address marks with missing clocks or CRC generation. The CRC generator is initialized when an F5 data byte is about to be transferred (in MFM). An F7 pattern will generate two CRC bytes. As a consequence, the patterns F5 thru FE must not appear in the gaps, data fiels, or ID fiels.

ImagineMyShock™ when I’ve seen a software that creates those sectors casually as if nothing happened. Once I’ve realized that it’s not only possible but it’s already been done by someone else, it took me entire 10 seconds of thinking to figure out how exactly they have done it: manipulate data prior to f7 in such a way that two bytes of CRC are exactly what you want to be written (in this case f7 01) - then just send f7 (write this 2 byte checksum) and finish by manually writing what should be a real CRC at this point.

In my experience, best way to deal with “unsolvable” problems is to try to get as close to solution as possible - in many cases “close enough” is well within your reach even if perfection is unattainable. In some cases, just by aiming for “good enough” you may overshoot and score perfection (that doesn’t happen all that often though). Just to demonstrate what I mean, here’re some of mathematically proven “impossible to solve” (at least within reasonable time) problems:

  • Optimizing compilers: register allocation could be reduced to graph coloring problem which is NP-hard. C++ type system is Turing-complete, which means typechecking in C++ is equivalent to halting problem. It’s not because C++ is “bad”, hipster languages like ML-family (ocaml, F#) and Haskell use EXPTIME-complete one which is not that much better in practice. Data-flow analysis in general is also equivalent to a halting problem, optimal code generation is exptime and so on. Yet, entire industry relies on those problems being solved “well enough”.
  • Games: from exptime-complete Chess and Go to even Candy Crush being np-hard. And those are easy ones - perfect information, reasonable amount of possible positions - for “hard” ones like Dota, Starcraft or Diablo I’m not aware of any formalism to even exist. Yet, not only humans like to solve those problems for entertainment - they even taught computers to do so.
  • VM/container orchestration (assigning VMs and/or containers to physical hosts) is NP-hard. Didn’t get in a way of AWS/Azure/GCP.

…and so on. Don’t get me wrong: there are cases where the only acceptable solution is the “perfect” one (your engine either conserves momentum or it doesn’t in which case you are just one of many), but in software engineering specifically it is rarely the case. Here we have many valid reason to not spend time on a project - that project being “impossible” is not one of them.

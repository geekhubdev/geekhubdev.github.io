Power of stories

Serge, 
September 8th, 2019 · 3 min read

We all live in stories. And I mean, we literally all live in stories. Some of you may object: Serge, you are literally a moron, who has no clue what literally means. Fair, but without invoking too much woo-woo can you remind me if there is any difference between you and some “primordial soup” where bajillion of different intricate chemical reactions take place, some of these reactions are even self-replicating and consuming resources? My bet is that the biggest difference is your brainzz, your consciousness, your psyche. And that thing lives in stories - no doubt about this. Being alive and conscious is qualitatively different from mere existence of atoms sharing electrons or forming hydrogen bonds.

So, what’s a story? They come in many names and forms: narratives, mindsets, mentalities, belief systems, self-images, identities, frames, etc.. Let’s unwrap it slowly and to start, remember how gps takes really long time to find your location initially, but then it’s able to give you live updates while on the road including moments when you are in a tunnel and there are no visible GPS satellites?

Another interesting tendency is that if you take a wrong turn, navigation app will continue to pretend that you are on the correct path until the evidence to the contrary is undeniable. What’s (roughly) going on there is that app keeps a model of where you should be at any given time and then if gps readings don’t contradict this prediction too much - it assumes that prediction is correct. If you think that you are so much more sophisticated than a navigation app - think again. Here’s a really old optical illusion:

Does it spin CW or CCW?.. Correct answer is it doesn’t spin at all - it’s just a blob of black pixels in 2d. Your brain creates a model of what would be the most plausible source of these pixels and then accepts this as a reality. In other words: you tell yourself that something spins there even though in the objective physical reality of particles and forces nothing spins or ever spinned to produce this image.

It says “your brain picks” instead of “you pick” because this process is mostly unconscious - conscious “you” has a very limited control over this process. Now, you probably know that this animation is bistable: model in your head can equally convincingly spin either CW or CCW, so try to change the direction you’ve seen initially… Tried? Getting off of a safe, familiar and predictable path of a story that has already successfully explained a shitton of events in your life and, most importantly, didn’t cause death by a saber-toothed mammoth is scary and anxiety-inducing, so it’s generally a good idea to avoid it. Uncertainty is always filled with all kinds of monsters: just remember medieval maps with “here be dragons” everywhere instead of “no clue”. On a flip side, it also means that it’s safer to stay on “Fat lazy loser Hwy” than to take an Exit 43 to a “You’ll probably definitely die Ave” no matter how many times you encounter a warning sign that this path ends in a cliffjump. Even if it didn’t get you this time, you definitely remember that time when it did. What’s going on here? Did I tell that your brain is constantly barraged by overwhelming amount of sensory input? Most of this input is irrelevant and is discarded as noise. Remember that time when you got numbed to a ticking clock? Uh, I can’t remember when was the last time I’ve seen a ticking clock. OK, then. Remember that time when you’ve been listening to an audiobook and then caught yourself in a thought that you don’t remember what was the last sentence about? Chances are, you’ve been concentrating on something else - crossing street, cooking food or writing tweet. Audiobook was irrelevant to the task at hand at that moment and therefore was ignored. By the same token when some information contradicts a story your brain tries to stick to - brain has a plausible deniability of just discarding that information as a noise. It gives us

But those contradictions don’t go completely unnoticed - they’ll produce a lingering feeling of annoyance that haunts you wherever you go. Of course you’ll try to resolve these dissonances. Here’s an example of how it might happen: if you believe that you are unlovable and somebody suddenly starts to love you, of course you will not update you beliefs. It’s DANGEROUS! You will first ignore all signs of affection towards you, then you’ll assign some ulterior motive to those signs such that you don’t have to change anything in your belief system, and then, if annoyance persists, you’ll drive it out of your life altogether and you’ll be very creative about it. Yay, you. You’ve successfully confirmed your beliefs - another notch on your belt (do not, I repeat, do not think about bunch of notch-es hanging from your belt). And the most depressing part? Your rationalizations and actions could be true. There are people who will use superficial charm and show affection to ultimately destroy you. Even if they don’t plan to destroy you right now - it might change down the road. It is safer to stay on the same path for the rest of your miserable life.
